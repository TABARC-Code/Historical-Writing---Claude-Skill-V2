# historical-fiction-master — Description

**Author:** TABARC-Code

---

## What It Does

This skill gives Claude the craft intelligence of a working historical fiction writer. Not just the vague awareness that “research matters” or that historical fiction exists as a genre. The useful stuff. The hard-won, practical stuff.

How to build a scene that feels as though it’s happening *inside* a past moment, not being viewed through museum glass. How to make a character whose fears, habits, beliefs, and blind spots belong to their own century, not ours. How to know when the research has done its job — and when it’s quietly become an excuse not to write.

It isn’t a content generator. It’s a craft system.

That distinction matters.

---

## The Core Doctrine

Everything in the skill grows out of one principle:

**once upon a time, it was now.**

Three hundred years ago, the 18th century wasn’t “the past”. It was now. People weren’t walking around inside history. They were trying to get through Tuesday. They had debts, toothache, hunger, weather, gossip, fear of God, fear of neighbours, fear of childbirth, fear of bad harvests. They didn’t know what came next. No one does, while they’re living it.

The historian stands in the 21st century and points backwards.

The historical novelist drags the reader into the past and makes them look forward.

Those are not the same act. They need different tools.

The governing word is **verisimilitude**. The appearance of truth. The felt truth. Which is often harder than accuracy, because the world has to feel real from the inside as well as survive contact with the record from the outside.

A book can be factually correct and still feel dead.

A book can take a few deliberate liberties and feel absolutely true.

Verisimilitude is what makes the difference.

This skill encodes the doctrine, decision rules, and corrective habits needed to write the second kind of book.

## The Ten Nodes

The skill is built around ten connected craft domains. Each has its own working doctrine. Each one leans into the others. You don’t move through them in a fixed order. You go where the manuscript is bleeding.

### **Immersion Engine**

The rules by which historical facts become lived experience.

The **Now Principle**: every scene must be written as though it’s happening to someone who doesn’t know what comes next.

The **White Heat Principle**: good historical prose comes from imaginative possession, not careful assembly. You can’t build a living scene by bolting facts together like flat-pack furniture.

The **Good Yucky Stuff Principle**: the biological, accidental, and grotesque details of historical life often do more work than three pages of polished description. Dirt under nails. Bad breath. Damp wool. Blood, lice, spoiled meat, old smoke in curtains. The particular carries authority.

The node also covers five-sense architecture. Sight gets overused. Smell is the strongest memory trigger and the one writers forget first. Every scene needs at least three senses active, with at least one non-visual sense doing real dramatic work.

Not garnish. Structure.

---

### **Research Architecture**

Research isn’t a phase. It’s a posture you keep through the whole project.

This node covers the **B.S. Filter** and the **Beagle Test**: the habit of stopping when something doesn’t quite make sense and digging until it does. Don’t just let the weird bit sit there and hope no one notices. They will. Or worse, they’ll feel it, even if they can’t name it.

It also treats communication speed as a plot problem, not a texture problem. A character can’t know something that hasn’t physically reached them yet. Letters, messengers, roads, weather, ports, literacy, censorship — these aren’t background details. Get them wrong and the plot breaks.

The node also handles over-research, which usually comes in two flavours.

The **Kudzu Problem**, where research grows over everything until there’s no story left.

And the **Flaubert Problem**, where the writer is so loyal to documented fact that fiction can’t breathe.

Both look respectable. Both can kill a novel.

---

### **Character Architecture**

This is character work treated like an actor preparing a role.

You need to know the character’s voice, body, habits, fears, class instincts, religious assumptions, and private embarrassments. What do they believe causes illness? What do they think fate is? Do they believe they can improve their social position, or would that idea sound ridiculous to them?

The body matters here. The body is an archive.

Scars, missing teeth, old injuries, limps, pregnancies, hunger, repeated labour, chronic pain, habitual gestures — all of it records history in flesh.

This node also includes specific protocols for illiterate protagonists and child narrators. Both are easy to get wrong. Most writers don’t notice till they’re already knee-deep in the mistake.

---

### **Language Craft**

This node covers period voice and anti-anachronism discipline.

The most damaging anachronisms usually aren’t objects. They’re attitudes.

A 17th-century character using modern therapeutic language to analyse their trauma is a bigger violation than a stray wristwatch. The watch is visible. The worldview is structural.

Language Craft also covers social codes and their consequences: duelling, honour, guild rules, religious duties, inheritance, marriage law, the legal position of women, class address, rank, taboo, shame.

These aren’t period decorations. They’re operating rules.

Characters have to move through them, dodge them, obey them, resent them, exploit them. Sometimes die by them.

---

### **Structure and Scope**

This node starts with the **gist question**:

What is this story about, in one sentence?

Not the plot summary. The real thing.

That sentence decides what research stays, what gets cut, where the book begins, where it ends, and what the reader is actually being asked to follow.

Structure and Scope also covers grabbers, endings, arc architecture, and the scope decision — when to begin and when to stop. Those choices are never arbitrary. Or shouldn’t be, anyway.

A historical novel that starts too early often becomes a guided tour.

One that starts too late may never let the reader feel the world before it cracks.

---

### **Credibility and Truth-Management**

This node deals with the old problem: where history stops and fiction begins.

The answer is: in every gap the record can’t close.

Invented scenes need to pass the verisimilitude test. Could this have happened? Does it fit the social, physical, political, emotional, and logistical world already established? Does it exploit a silence in the record, or does it contradict the record because the plot needed a shortcut?

Dialogue fabrication has its own rules. It should be grounded in letters, journals, political concerns, documented speech patterns, and the pressures of the moment — not just in whatever sounds good to us.

This node also includes the **Then-and-Now obligation**: the requirement that a historical novel illuminate the present through the past.

Not as a lecture. Never as a lecture.

It has to be built into the architecture.

---

### **The Audit Protocol**

This is the twelve-point review system for any draft.

It includes:

* anachronism sweeps across objects, language, belief, and communication technology
* B.S. Filter checks
* sensory audit
* verisimilitude checks for invented material
* immersion-break audit
* cliché and stereotype sweep
* period texture spot-check
* Then-and-Now resonance check
* rosy past and sanitisation sweep
* historical cruelty and stakes check
* research quality review
* over-research warning

The Audit Protocol isn’t paperwork. It’s where all the other nodes meet with knives out.

---

### **The Rosy Past Problem**

A lot of people want to write about a period because they’re in love with an image of it.

Childhood books. National mythology. Costume drama. Family stories. Folk horror. Pretty houses. Candlelight. A map with dragons in the margins.

Fine. That’s often where the impulse starts.

But the image has to be diagnosed before the draft begins. Not apologised for later in the author’s note.

This node includes the **Nostalgia Audit**, the **Sanitisation Prohibition**, the **Warts and All Imperative**, and the **Axe Audit**.

The Axe Audit asks: what do you *want* to be true about this period, and where is that desire likely to distort the record?

That question is uncomfortable. Which is why it’s useful.

Characters must hold the beliefs of their era, including the abhorrent ones. That doesn’t mean endorsing those beliefs. It means not quietly replacing the past with a modern conscience in antique clothes.

---

### **Embodied Research**

This node gets the writer away from the keyboard.

Go to the site. Touch the tool. Walk the hill. Handle the document. Smell the church. Stand in the room and notice where the light actually falls.

The **Peripheral Vision Principle** says the most useful discoveries are usually tangential. You find them while looking for something else. Send a proxy and you’ll get the requested fact, maybe. You won’t get the strange little detail in the corner that turns into a scene.

Embodied Research also covers re-enactor consultation, the **Tool Principle**, and primary document contact.

The Tool Principle is simple: learn how the period implement works before you write someone using it.

A scythe, a musket, a loom, a quill, a horse harness, a washboard. They all shape the body. They all create tempo. You can’t fake that forever.

---

### **Historical Cruelty and Social Control**

Most historical periods were governed by systems of punishment, terror, exposure, shame, poverty, and bodily risk that modern writers tend to soften without noticing.

This node keeps those systems in the air.

The cost of defiance was usually not a stern conversation. It might be hunger, whipping, transportation, branding, confinement, rape, exile, hanging, ruin, or the destruction of your family’s prospects.

That doesn’t mean every scene needs violence. It means characters should know what world they’re in.

This node also covers the rendering of historical perpetrators as specific human beings operating within systems, rather than cardboard villains.

That isn’t exculpation.

It’s more honest. And usually more disturbing.

## The Two New Reference Files

### **Sentiment Archaeology**

Sentiment Archaeology is about recovering emotional truth from fragmentary evidence.

History records events. It rarely records how someone felt at three in the morning before the day that changed their life.

The novelist’s job isn’t to invent the inner life of the past out of thin air. It’s to reconstruct it.

You work backwards from the *petits faits vrais* — the little true facts. The concrete details too specific to feel fabricated. You look at gaps in the record, damaged objects, marginal notes, repairs, omissions, clothing, household goods, burial choices, legal phrasing, devotional habits.

Then you ask three questions:

1. What would they actually have felt?
2. What vocabulary did they have for it?
3. What would they have done with it?

The third question matters most.

A 17th-century Puritan doesn’t experience grief through modern psychological language. They may understand it through divine testing, original sin, providence, sin, duty, bodily weakness, and the expected brevity of life before eternity.

Same human wound. Different map of the wound.

---

### **Embodied Research**

Embodied Research is the full protocol for physical, site-based, and primary-document research.

It covers what to do at a historical site once you’ve finished staring at the obvious thing. Where are the draughts? What can’t you see from the doorway? How far is the well? Where would sound carry? What would stink in summer? What would freeze first?

It also covers how to approach archivists, how to work with re-enactor communities, and how to judge digital sources.

The digital research reliability gradient runs from digitised institutional primary sources at the top, down through academic work, museum databases, specialist local history, commercial summaries, blogs, forums, and social media.

Wikipedia sits where it should: useful as a bibliographic starting point. Not an authority.

This file also covers the stability problem — online material changes, disappears, mutates, and gets copied with its errors intact. Corrections crawl. Mistakes sprint.

Which is annoying, but there we are.

## Who It Is For

This skill is for writers working on historical fiction who want sharper instincts and fewer blind spots.

It’s for researchers who know the period but don’t yet know how to make it live on the page.

It’s for writers who’ve been told their protagonist feels anachronistic and don’t know exactly why.

It’s for writers who’ve been researching for two years and still can’t start the manuscript.

It’s for writers whose draft feels like a guided tour of the archive rather than a novel.

And, yes, it’s also for writers who already know what they’re doing and want a system beside them that knows what it’s doing too.

It is not for people who want historical fiction produced on demand without craft engagement.

The skill works with you.

Not instead of you.

That’s the design. That’s the point.
